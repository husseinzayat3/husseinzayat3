Vibe Coding: Fast Prototypes, Fragile Foundations

Oct 2, 2025 · 2 min read

Over the past weeks, I experimented with vibe coding while building a simple app with complex logic. It’s fun and it definitely compresses time-to-prototype — but it also reinforced a timeless lesson: speed without structure is expensive later.

What works well

  • Quick exploration of ideas and UX flows.
  • Unblocking unknowns: “Can this even work?”
  • Generating momentum for stakeholders and teams.

Where it bites back

  • Hidden coupling and unclear boundaries.
  • Weak tests and flaky behavior under change.
  • Onboarding friction: the next developer slows down.

The balance that works for me

  • Timebox the prototype — agree it will be thrown away or hardened.
  • Capture decisions — note trade-offs and assumptions as you go.
  • Stabilize early — add tests, interfaces, and clear module contracts.
  • Refactor to patterns — before adding features, make the shape right.

For me, vibe coding is a booster, not a destination. It shines when used to learn fast — and then replaced (or refactored) with code that’s maintainable, testable, and ready for a team.

When Everyone Can Code, What Makes an Engineer?

Oct 7, 2025 · 4 min read

In the age of AI, it feels like everyone can be a developer. With a few prompts, we can generate working code, scaffold entire applications, or even automate documentation. It’s exciting — and a little bit surreal — to see how far we’ve come.

But as someone who’s spent years building backend systems, designing architectures, and leading engineering teams, I’ve learned that writing code has never been the hardest part of software development. AI has made coding easier — but engineering is still about something deeper.

The difference between code and engineering

Writing code is an act of creation. Engineering is an act of design. A good engineer doesn’t just make a feature work — they make it fit into a system that others can understand, maintain, and extend. They think about scalability, performance, and failure scenarios long before they happen.

Where AI helps — and where it doesn’t

AI can now write decent code, generate unit tests, and even propose optimizations. It’s an incredible assistant. But what AI still struggles with is context — understanding trade-offs, designing boundaries, and thinking in systems rather than snippets. Our job is shifting from writing code to orchestrating intelligence — both human and artificial — to build reliable and meaningful software.

The human side of engineering

Good engineers solve problems. Great engineers make it easier for others to solve them. When everyone can write code, the value shifts to those who can architect, govern, and scale it sustainably. AI won’t replace that anytime soon.

Closing thought

The future belongs to those who can blend technical precision with human judgment. Code may be generated by machines — but engineering will always be a human art.

Impressed by Google Antigravity: A Developer’s Perspective After Two Weeks

Dec 12, 2025 · 3 min read

Over the past couple of weeks, I’ve been using Google Antigravity, and I didn’t expect it to leave this kind of impression on me. I try a lot of AI tools for development, but this is the first one that genuinely made me pause and rethink how AI fits into my daily workflow.

What makes Antigravity stand out isn’t just speed or code generation — it’s understanding. It feels less like a tool that responds to prompts and more like a system that understands intent.

Where It Really Shines: Frontend Development

Frontend development is where Google Antigravity truly excels. From layout structure to UI flow, it consistently produces results that feel intentional and well thought out. Turning an idea into a working interface happens incredibly fast, and the quality is surprisingly high.

Instead of fighting boilerplate or repetitive UI tasks, I found myself focusing more on product decisions and user experience — which is exactly where developers should spend their time.

Backend: Promising, but Still Evolving

On the backend side, Antigravity isn’t perfect yet. Some areas still need refinement, and you can tell it’s an evolving product. That said, the foundation is clearly there. With continued improvements, it has the potential to become a strong full-stack companion rather than just a frontend powerhouse.

Why This Matters

What impressed me most is how Antigravity changes the feeling of development:

  • Less friction
  • Faster experimentation
  • More room for creativity
  • Less mental fatigue

It’s one of the first AI tools that made building things feel genuinely enjoyable again.

Final Thoughts

Google Antigravity feels like a glimpse into where AI-assisted development is heading. It’s not perfect — and that’s okay — but it already delivers real value, especially for frontend work.

If you’re a developer, product builder, or someone who enjoys creating software, this is absolutely worth trying. I didn’t expect to be this impressed, but here we are.
